Generation II

The Children of Dolor Davis

New England, 1626–1713  ·  Five families, one enduring name

Dolor and Margery raised five children who survived to adulthood, each founding a family of their own across the Cape, the South Shore, and the Massachusetts interior. This generation is the last born in the Old World and the first to grow up entirely as New Englanders.

The Horace Davis Line · Through Samuel
SD
Samuel Davis
birth unknown, America  ·  died after 1714, Bedford, Massachusetts
Horace Line Samuel Davis is the direct ancestor of Horace Davis, the author of the 1881 chronicle. The line descends: Samuel → Simon I (Lieut.) → Simon II → Simon III → Isaac → John → Horace Davis.
unknownBirth
Born in America
Samuel's exact birth date is unrecorded. He was born in America — likely in Duxbury or Barnstable during his father's years in Plymouth Colony, and was perhaps still an infant when the family moved to Barnstable around 1640. He and his siblings Simon I and Ruth were the New World children of Dolor and Margery, growing up entirely in colonial Massachusetts. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 40.
Jan. 11,
1666
Marriage to Mary Meddowes
Samuel married Mary Meddowes at Lynn, Massachusetts on January 11, 1666 — while his father was still living in Concord. The marriage record appears both on the Concord records and independently on the records of Lynn.
First Wife
Mary Meddowes Davis
d. at Concord, October 3, 1710

Mary bore Samuel seven children, all recorded. She died in Concord, outliving the couple's early years in what would become the town of Bedford — the land Dolor had settled upon them.
Oct. 18,
1711
Second Marriage to Ruth Taylor
After Mary's death in 1710, Samuel married a second time — Ruth Taylor — on October 18, 1711. Ruth died on August 6, 1720. There were no children by this second marriage. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 40.
Mar. 21,
1690
Admitted Freeman of Massachusetts Colony
Samuel was admitted as a freeman of Massachusetts Colony on March 21, 1690 — reflecting his established standing in the community, though Horace Davis notes he took little part in public affairs compared to his more prominent brother Simon. In his deeds he styled himself husbandman and yeoman. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 40.
c. 1655
onward
Settlement in Bedford (from Concord)
Samuel settled on the portion of Concord land that his father Dolor granted him — land that would eventually become the separate town of Bedford, Massachusetts. His homestead lay on the back road from Concord to Bedford, about three miles from the Concord village center, on the edge of the river meadow.

Horace Davis visited this farm in person and found it still owned by Mr. Benjamin J. Davis, a direct descendant, in unbroken family possession. A well on the property is said to have been dug by Samuel himself.
Bedford, Massachusetts — carved from the western reaches of Concord, this was Samuel Davis's home and the land he farmed on the meadow's edge.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 40–41.
by
1714
Final Years & Death
Ancient deeds in the possession of Mr. Benjamin J. Davis showed Samuel still living as of March 15, 1714. By that time he had already conveyed most of his land to his sons during his own lifetime — a practice he inherited from his father Dolor, who had done the same. The exact date and place of his burial are unknown. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 40.

The Other Children of Dolor Davis
JD
John Davis
b. c. 1626, England  ·  d. 1703, Barnstable, Massachusetts
Mar. 15,
1649
Marriage & Life in Barnstable
The eldest son of Dolor, John came to New England as a boy of nine aboard the Elizabeth in 1635. He married Hannah Lynnell, daughter of Robert Lynnell of Barnstable, on March 15, 1649. He remained in Barnstable all his life as a farmer and house-carpenter — the same trade as his father. His name appears frequently in the Barnstable town records in connection with land transfers, negotiations with local Indians, and civic service. He served as surveyor of highways for many years and was sole executor of his father's will in 1673.
A reconstructed Plymouth Colony homestead — the type of dwelling John Davis would have built and occupied as a house-carpenter in Barnstable.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 33–34.
May 10,
1701
Will & Death in 1703
John's will, dated May 10, 1701, names his wife as living, and lists six sons — John, Benjamin, Samuel, Dollar, Timothy, and Jabez — and daughters Mercy, Ruth Lynnell, and Mary Hincley. He died in 1703; the inventory of his estate totalled £268 12s. 4d. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 33.

Marriage to Thomas Lewis
Mary Davis, the second child of Dolor and Margery, was married at Barnstable by Thomas Hincley to Thomas Lewis, son of George Lewis, on June 15, 1653. Thomas was born in England, was a freeman of Plymouth Colony from 1658, and later removed to Falmouth, where he served as proprietors' clerk and was a prominent citizen. Mary was noted still living in 1672. The couple had four children. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 37.

SD
Simon Davis I, Lieut.
b. c. 1636, America  ·  d. June 14, 1713, Concord, Massachusetts
Dec. 12,
1660
Marriage & Settlement in Concord
Simon, the first child born to Dolor and Margery in America, married Mary Blood, daughter of James Blood of Concord, on December 12, 1660 — while still living in his father's household. He settled in Concord on land Dolor granted him, and remained there all his life.
Wife
Mary Blood Davis
b. July 12, 1640 · d. after 1713, Concord
Daughter of James Blood of Concord. Mary was still living when Simon made his will in 1710, and is mentioned therein along with all seven surviving children.
1675
King Philip's War — Military Command
Simon Davis earned his rank as Lieutenant through military service in King Philip's War (1675–76), the devastating conflict between English colonists and the Wampanoag Confederacy. After the wounding of Captain Wheeler at Brookfield in August 1675, Simon assumed command of the troops — a moment of genuine colonial historical significance. He was elected Deputy to the Massachusetts General Court in 1689, 1690, 1692, and 1705, and held other positions of civic responsibility.
"The Puritan" by Augustus Saint-Gaudens — the archetype of the determined New England colonist of Simon's era, a man of faith, land, and civic duty.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 38.
June 14,
1713
Death at Concord, aged 77
Simon Davis I died on June 14, 1713, at approximately 77 years of age. His gravestone still stands in the Concord burying-ground. His original will, made June 24, 1710, survives at East Cambridge in his own autograph — a finer specimen of his hand may also be seen on the Concord records of 1689, attesting the election of town clerk. Like his father and brother Samuel, he had already divided most of his real estate among his sons before his death. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 38.

Notable Descendant · Simon Line
Captain Isaac Davis
February 23, 1745 – April 19, 1775  ·  Acton, Massachusetts
Simon I's eldest son, Dr. Simon Davis, had a son named Dr. John Davis (b. 1689, Concord), who had a son named Ezekiel Davis (b. 1717, Concord), who settled in Acton and became the father of Captain Isaac Davis — making Isaac Dolor Davis's great-great-great-grandson, and a distant cousin of Horace Davis.

Isaac Davis was a gunsmith and captain of the Acton Minutemen. On the morning of April 19, 1775, he led his men to Concord's Old North Bridge — his company was chosen to advance first because they alone were fully equipped with bayonets. He was shot through the heart in the opening volley, becoming the first American officer killed in the Revolutionary War. His last words to his wife before marching were: "Take good care of the children."

Isaac Davis was the inspiration for The Minute Man (1875), the bronze sculpture by Daniel Chester French at the Old North Bridge — now one of the most iconic images in American history. The sculptor modeled the figure using photographs of Davis's own descendants.
The Minute Man (1875) by Daniel Chester French, Old North Bridge, Concord, Massachusetts — modeled after Isaac Davis and his descendants. Cast from Civil War cannons; unveiled April 19, 1875, before President Grant, Longfellow, and Emerson.

Ruth Davis Hall
bapt. March 24, 1645, Barnstable  ·  date of death unknown
Dec. 3,
1663
Marriage to Stephen Hall & Migrations
Ruth, the youngest child of Dolor and Margery, was baptized at Barnstable on March 24, 1645 — the only child whose New World baptism was formally entered by Rev. John Lothrop. She married Stephen Hall of Concord on December 3, 1663, while the family was still in Concord. Stephen Hall later removed to Stow (1685), served as representative there in 1689, then moved to Medford, and finally in 1699 to Connecticut. Ruth and Stephen had six children. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 43.

Five children of Dolor Davis survived to adulthood, married, and founded families across New England — from Cape Cod to Concord to Connecticut. Together they produced at least thirty-six children of their own, spreading the Davis name through Plymouth Colony and Massachusetts Bay. It was Samuel's line, settled on the Bedford meadows, that would carry the name forward through the generations toward Horace Davis and beyond.

— Compiled from Horace Davis (1881)
