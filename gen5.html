Generation V

Isaac Davis

Of Westborough & Northborough, Massachusetts  ·  1749–1826
Dolor Davis→ Samuel Davis→ Simon Davis II→ Simon Davis III→ Isaac Davis (b. 1749)→ John Davis→ Horace Davis

Fifth in the Horace line, Isaac Davis was born into a Massachusetts on the eve of revolution, orphaned before he was twelve, and came of age in the new republic. He became a deacon, a farmer, a legislator, and the father of a Governor — the first member of the direct Horace line to leave a mark on American public life.

A Note on Names · Two Isaac Davises
Not the Soldier — A Distant Cousin
Captain Isaac Davis (1745–1775)  ·  Isaac Davis (1749–1826)
There are two Isaac Davises in this family — born just four years apart, both great-great-great-grandsons of Dolor Davis, living in neighboring Massachusetts towns at the same time. The first is Captain Isaac Davis of Acton (b. 1745), who commanded the Minutemen at the Battle of Concord on April 19, 1775, and was killed in the opening volley — the first American officer to die in the Revolutionary War. He descended through Dolor's son Simon I.

The Isaac Davis on this page (b. 1749) is a different man entirely — descended through Dolor's son Samuel, and the direct ancestor of Horace Davis. He was a civilian, a farmer and deacon in Northborough, and a legislator. He was 26 years old when his cousin fell at Concord — almost certainly aware of the connection, perhaps even acquainted with him. He lived another fifty-one years after that April morning, long enough to see the nation his cousin died for become a functioning republic.

Horace Line · Generation V Isaac is the ninth child of Simon Davis III and Hannah Gates, and the fifth-generation descendant of Dolor Davis. He is the first in the direct Horace line to hold elected public office — seven terms in the Massachusetts Legislature — and the father of Governor John Davis.
Feb. 27,
1749
Birth in Rutland — Orphaned by Age Twelve
Isaac Davis was born on February 27, 1749 in Rutland, Massachusetts — the ninth of eleven children of Simon Davis III and Hannah Gates. He was just five years old when his father died in April 1754, and twelve when his mother Hannah died in January 1761. He came of age, then, largely without parents, in the household of older siblings in Rutland and the surrounding Worcester County towns.
Historical Context · Isaac's Youth
Isaac's childhood unfolded against the backdrop of the French and Indian War (1754–1763) — begun the very year his father died. By the time he was a young man of twenty, the Stamp Act crisis had come and gone, the Boston Massacre had occurred (1770), and the colonies were moving inexorably toward open conflict. Isaac Davis came of age in precisely the generation shaped by revolutionary feeling. He was 26 years old on April 19, 1775, the day his distant cousin Captain Isaac Davis of Acton was killed at Concord.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 44–45.
circa1770
Removal to Westborough
As a young man, Isaac removed from Rutland to Westborough, Massachusetts — a town in Worcester County about twenty miles to the southeast, closer to the more prosperous commercial corridor of the region. It was there that he established himself as a farmer and community member, and where he married his first wife Anna Brigham. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 44.
May 21,
1772
First Marriage — Anna Brigham
Isaac married Anna Brigham of Westborough on May 21, 1772. She had been born on October 29, 1753, the daughter of Samuel Brigham of Westborough. They had eleven children together before Anna's death on April 24, 1803.

Anna bore Isaac eleven children over more than two decades. She died in 1803, two years after the family's removal to Northborough. Their son John Davis — the future Governor — was born in 1787, the year the U.S. Constitution was written.

1781
Removal to Northborough
In 1781 — the year of Yorktown, the decisive final battle of the Revolution — Isaac Davis moved his family from Westborough to the neighboring township of Northborough, Massachusetts, where the family would remain. Northborough had been incorporated as a separate town from Westborough only the year before, in 1780, making Isaac one of its earliest residents. It was here that he farmed, served his church as deacon, and built the civic life that would define the rest of his years.
Northborough, Massachusetts — incorporated in 1780, it was Isaac Davis's home from 1781 until his death in 1826. He was among its founding residents.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 44.
early
1800s
Ordained Deacon
Isaac Davis served his church in Northborough as deacon — a lay leadership role in the Congregational tradition that carried significant community standing. The Davis family had been active in Congregational churches since Dolor himself joined the church at Duxbury in the 1640s, and the deaconship represented a continuation of that deep religious commitment now six generations on. Isaac's brother David in Paxton and his brother Samuel in Oakham both also held the rank of deacon — a remarkable family pattern. Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 44–45.
seven
terms
Seven Terms in the Massachusetts Legislature
Isaac Davis served seven terms as a Representative in the Massachusetts Legislature — the first member of the direct Horace line to hold elected office at the state level. His service spanned the early decades of the republic, when Massachusetts was actively shaping the laws and institutions of the new nation. The exact years of his service are not recorded in the Horace Davis chronicle, but given his dates and his son John's later career, his legislative terms likely fell in the 1790s–1810s.
The Massachusetts State House, Boston — completed in 1798, this is where Isaac Davis served his seven legislative terms as representative for Northborough.
Worcester County, Massachusetts — the political and civic world in which Isaac Davis served as deacon, farmer, and legislator for fifty years.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 44.

Apr. 27,
1826
Death at Northborough, aged 77
Isaac Davis died on April 27, 1826, in Northborough, at the age of 77. He had outlived the Revolution, the Constitutional Convention, the War of 1812, and the beginning of the Erie Canal era. His son John Davis — then 39 years old and serving in Congress — would go on to become Governor of Massachusetts and a United States Senator. His grandson Horace Davis was not yet born.

Isaac had lived from the eve of the Revolution to the age of John Quincy Adams's presidency — a span that encompassed the entire founding era of the American republic. The immigrant Dolor had arrived on these shores nearly two centuries before; his fifth-generation descendant died a citizen of a nation that hadn't existed when Dolor was born. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 44.
