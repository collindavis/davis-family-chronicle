Generation IV

Simon Davis III

Of Rutland, Massachusetts  ·  1714–1754
Dolor Davis→ Samuel Davis→ Simon Davis II→ Simon Davis III (b. 1714)→ Isaac Davis→ John Davis→ Horace Davis

Fourth in the Horace line, Simon Davis III was born into a Massachusetts on the cusp of change — the frontier wars of his father's era giving way to a more settled colonial life. He lived entirely in Rutland, married Hannah Gates of Stow, fathered eleven children, and died young at forty — leaving his family to carry on the Davis name into the era of Revolution.

Simon Davis III
born May 17, 1714, Rutland, Massachusetts  ·  died April 9, 1754, Rutland, Massachusetts
Horace Line · Generation IV Simon III is the eldest son of Simon Davis II and Dorothy, and great-great-grandson of Dolor Davis. He is the last of the three consecutive Simons in the direct Horace line. His son Isaac Davis (b. 1749) carries the name into the Northborough branch and toward the Governor's chair.
May 17,
1714
Birth in Rutland, Massachusetts
Simon III was born on May 17, 1714 in Rutland, Massachusetts — the same frontier township to which his father Simon II had moved the family just a few years earlier. Rutland had been incorporated only that same year, making Simon III among its very first native-born residents. He was the eldest of Simon II and Dorothy's eight children, and grew up as Rutland slowly transformed from a raw settlement into an established Worcester County town.
Historical Context · 1714
Simon III's birth year coincides with the end of Queen Anne's War (1702–1713) — the second of the French and Indian Wars — which had kept the Massachusetts interior under near-constant threat of French and Native raid. The Treaty of Utrecht in 1713 brought a generation of relative peace to New England, and Simon III grew up in that more settled world. His childhood spanned the era when towns like Rutland shifted from palisaded garrisons to ordinary farming communities.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 42. Rutland town records.
circa1733
Marriage to Hannah Gates
Simon III married Hannah Gates of Stow, Massachusetts. The exact date of marriage is not recorded in the available sources, but their first child appears in the records around 1735, placing the marriage around 1733. Hannah was from Stow — a town in Middlesex County, about twenty-five miles east of Rutland — suggesting the family maintained connections across the central Massachusetts region even as they put down roots in Worcester County.

Hannah Gates Davis
b. unknown, Stow, Massachusetts  ·  d. January 7, 1761, aged 46
Buried in Holden graveyard alongside Simon III.

Hannah outlived her husband by seven years, dying on January 7, 1761, in the 46th year of her age. She raised their eleven children — the youngest, Isaac, was just five years old when his father died and twelve when his mother followed. Of all the Davis wives in the Horace line, Hannah Gates is the one Horace Davis singled out by name in his most personal note: "They are my ancestors."
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 42.
1714
–1754
Life in Rutland
Simon III spent his entire life in Rutland. Unlike his father Simon II, who had made the move from Bedford, or his grandfather Samuel, who had farmed the Concord meadows — Simon III was of the first generation to know only one place his whole life. The Davis family was now genuinely settled, four generations removed from the immigrant Dolor who had moved restlessly across six towns.

Like his father and grandfather, Simon III would have been a farmer and landowner — a husbandman by the language of the deeds. Rutland in the 1720s–1750s was a growing agricultural community, its early hardships behind it, its meetinghouse and common established. The Davis family was among its founding families, and Simon III would have been a recognized and rooted member of the community.
The historic center of Rutland, Massachusetts — Simon III's lifelong home, where he was born, married, raised eleven children, and died.
Central Massachusetts c. 1750, the era of Simon III's adult life — Rutland and its Worcester County neighbors now a generation removed from the frontier.

–1752
Eleven Children in Rutland
Simon III and Hannah had eleven children over roughly seventeen years — a large family even by colonial standards. Their children spread across the Worcester County towns of Rutland, Holden, Paxton, Leicester, Sterling, and Westborough as they came of age. Of all these children, it is the ninth — Isaac Davis, born February 27, 1749 — who carries the Horace line forward into the next chapter of the family's story. Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 44–45.
Apr. 9,
1754
Death at Rutland, aged 39
Simon III died on April 9, 1754, in Rutland, at just 39 years of age — the youngest death in the direct Horace line so far. The cause of his death is unrecorded. He left Hannah with eleven children ranging in age from approximately nineteen years to five years old. His youngest son Isaac was only five at the time.
An Early Loss
Simon III's early death at 39 means he never saw the French and Indian War's conclusion (1763), the Stamp Act crisis (1765), or the revolutionary ferment that would define his children's generation. His son Isaac — who would become a deacon and seven-term legislator — was still an infant. The widow Hannah Gates raised the family alone for seven more years until her own death in 1761, leaving the children, some still young, to make their own way. It is a testament to the family's resilience that the line not only survived but flourished.
The Holden graveyard — where Simon III and Hannah Gates Davis are both buried, their gravestones among the earliest physical Davis family monuments in Worcester County.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 42.

Of Simon III and Hannah Gates, Horace Davis wrote simply and movingly: "They are my ancestors." In a chronicle otherwise filled with dates, deeds, and genealogical detail, that plain statement carries a particular weight — the direct acknowledgment of the living thread connecting the author to this quiet Rutland farmer and his wife.

— Horace Davis, "Dolor Davis: A Sketch of His Life" (1881), p. 42

Into the Age of Revolution
Simon III died in 1754, the year before the French and Indian War began. His eleven children came of age in the turbulent decades that followed — the Stamp Act crisis, the Boston Massacre, the Tea Act, and finally the outbreak of revolution in 1775. His son Isaac Davis (b. 1749) was twenty-six years old when the shots were fired at Lexington and Concord. His other son John (b. 1752) served as a colonel in the militia. The generation Simon III left behind was the generation of the American Revolution.

Of particular note: his cousin Captain Isaac Davis of Acton — descended from Simon I through the Dr. Simon Davis branch — was killed at the North Bridge on April 19, 1775, the first American officer to die in the Revolutionary War. The Davis family, across its two surviving lines from Dolor, was present at the very founding of the nation.
