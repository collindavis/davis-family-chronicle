The Davis Family Chronicle
Generation I

Dolor Davis

Of Kent & Sussex, England & New England — born before 1593, died June 1673

He was not a man of great station, but of great endurance — a house carpenter and farmer who crossed an ocean, carved a home from wilderness, and planted a family that would flourish for generations across the New World.

DD
Dolor Davis
born before 1593, Kent / Sussex, England  ·  died June 1673, Barnstable, Massachusetts
before1593
Birth in Kent, England
Dolor Davis was born before 1593 — almost certainly earlier than the long-assumed estimate of c.1599. The key evidence is a 1614 will: James Clarke of East Farleigh, Kent, bequeathed to his servant Dolor Davis a house and land in the parish of Marden. To inherit property outright from an employer, Dolor must have been over 21 at that time, placing his birth no later than 1593 and possibly earlier. His precise birthplace has never been established; his last known English residence was Catsfield, Sussex — not Kent as previously assumed — where parish records place the family in the early 1630s. The Weald of Kent, however, remains the most likely region of his origins, where the Davis name was especially common.
The Weald, Kent — the wooded district of England where the Davis name was most common, and likely home to Dolor's origins.
Source: Horace Davis, "Dolor Davis: A Sketch of His Life" (1881), pp. 4–5.
note onName
A Singular Name
The name Dolor appears in New England records under at least a dozen spellings — Dolar, Dolard, Dolord, Dolore, Dollard, Dolerd, Dollar, Doller — yet its origin remains unknown. The family's chosen form was Dolor, and it was pronounced "Doller," as preserved in the oral tradition of Barnstable for several generations. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 5.
July 13,
1614
A Servant Who Inherited Land
The earliest documented record of Dolor Davis is a will. On July 13, 1614, James Clarke of East Farleigh, Kent bequeathed to his servant Dolor Davis his house and land in the parish of Marden. This was a remarkable bequest — property left not to family but to a trusted servant, suggesting Dolor had made a deep impression on his employer. At this time Dolor was at least 21 years old, working as a tradesman in the East Farleigh area of Kent. Interestingly, another servant of Clarke's named Abraham Preble of Wooton, Kent, also appears in the will — and Preble too became an early New England immigrant, suggesting the same employer's household produced two future colonists. Source: NEHGR, Vol. 74, p. 70. WikiTree Davis-187 (updated January 2026).
circa1624
Marriage to Margery Willard
Dolor married Margery Willard, daughter of Richard Willard of Horsemonden, Kent — a yeoman landowner who left a considerable estate upon his death in 1617. Margery had been christened on November 7, 1602, her record still visible today in the parish register of Horsemonden. She was approximately twenty-two years of age at marriage.
First Wife
Margery Willard Davis
b. November 7, 1602, Horsemonden, Kent, England
d. between 1658 and 1667, Concord, Massachusetts

Daughter of Richard Willard (yeoman). Her brothers Simon and George Willard also emigrated to New England. The only mention of Margery by name in original New World documents is the deed of 1658 in Concord, wherein she and Dolor jointly acknowledged a land conveyance before Simon Willard.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 5–7. The Willard Memoir by Joseph Willard provides additional family detail.
1626
–1634
Two Children Born in England
Before crossing the Atlantic, two children were born to Dolor and Margery in England: John and Mary — though the record is more complicated than it first appears. Parish records at Catsfield, Sussex show a first daughter named Mary christened on May 9, 1631, who apparently died very young. The name was reused: a second Mary was christened on February 2, 1633/34, also at Catsfield — and this second Mary is the one who came to America and eventually married Thomas Lewis. So there were effectively three England-born children, but only John and Mary II survived to emigrate.

1634
Dolor Arrives in Cambridge, Massachusetts
Dolor sailed to New England in 1634 — likely alongside his brother-in-law Simon Willard, who landed at Boston in May of that year. He came ahead of his family. On August 4, 1634, the town records of Cambridge record the first evidence of his presence: "Granted Dollard Davis there 25 acres" — a parcel on the west side of the Charles River.
A 17th-century vessel of the type that carried Puritan settlers across the Atlantic — Dolor likely sailed in 1634 alongside Simon Willard.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 7–8. Cambridge Town Records, August 4, 1634.
Apr. 17,
1635
Margery & the Children Cross the Atlantic
Having secured his land grant, Dolor sent for his family. Margery and the two children sailed from London aboard the Elizabeth, Captain William Stagg — one of seventy-six passengers, a striking proportion traced to Kent. The ship's manifest records: Margaret Davies, aged 32; John Davies, 9; and Marie Davies, 4.

The manifest also lists an Elizabeth Davies, age 1 — a name that has led some genealogists to count Elizabeth as a third child born in England. Subsequent research has resolved this conclusively. Dr. Frank Mike Davis researched parishes in Bexhill and Catsfield extensively, employing a professional researcher at the Keep Library in Sussex, and found no trace of a daughter Elizabeth in any English record. Anderson's Great Migration Directory (2015) acknowledged the "Elizabeth Davies" entry was an error — the passengers were Dolor's family, and no daughter Elizabeth existed. She has been removed from this chronicle accordingly. Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 7–8.
June 4,
1635
House-Lot Established in Cambridge
With his family arrived, Dolor recorded his grants in the Proprietors' Records: a house-lot of half a rood on Water Street (now Dunster Street), lying between Harvard College and the Charles River. He likely built a dwelling-house on the lot. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 8. Cambridge Proprietors' Records, June 4, 1635.
Aug. 25,
1635
Departs Cambridge — Sells His Land
Cambridge in 1635–36 was fractured by religious dissension; over half its settlers left, many following Rev. Hooker to Hartford. Dolor sold his 25-acre lot to Richard Girling, mariner, for ten pounds, disposed of his house-lot to Willard, and turned south toward Plymouth Colony. Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 9–10.
May 3,
1641
Standing Surety for a Willard
On May 3, 1641, described in the Plymouth Colony records as a planter of Scituate, Dolor Davis gave a surety bond to the Plymouth Colony Court on behalf of George Willard — his wife Margery's brother — who had spoken disrespectfully of the colonial magistrates. It was an act of loyalty to the Willard family that Dolor had known since his days in Kent, and it cost him nothing but his standing. That he was willing to put himself on the line legally for a brother-in-law in trouble says something real about the bonds that crossed an ocean with this family. Source: Records of the Colony of New Plymouth, vol. 2, p. 14.
1636
–1640
Duxbury, Plymouth Colony
Dolor moved his family to Duxbury in Plymouth Colony between 1636 and 1638, taking the required oath of fidelity. He was propounded for freeman on March 5, 1639, joined the Duxbury church, and was granted land twice by the Court of Assistants in 1640 — including fifty acres on the Namassacusset River. Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 10–11. Plymouth Colony Records, vols. i and viii.
1639
–1655
Settlement at Barnstable, Cape Cod
Rev. John Lothrop's congregation from Scituate founded Barnstable in 1639, drawing settlers largely from Kent, England — which almost certainly drew Dolor to their company. He was listed among the inhabitants in 1640, permanent by 1642 or 1643. He served as surveyor of highways (1652) and constable (1654), and became a freeman of Plymouth Colony on June 2, 1646. He acquired over 120 acres in and around Barnstable, with his house standing close to the ancient water-mill.
Barnstable's Great Marsh, Cape Cod — the landscape Dolor looked out upon from his house near the mill, very much unchanged from his time.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 12–16.

Three More Children Born in America
Three children were born to Dolor and Margery in the New World. The youngest, Ruth, is the only child whose baptism was formally recorded — entered by Rev. John Lothrop into the Barnstable Church records on March 24, 1645.

1655
–1666
Move to Concord, Massachusetts
In 1655, Dolor sold his Barnstable properties and moved his family to Concord, Massachusetts. He purchased the Roger Draper farm — 152 acres including house and outbuildings — and retained it until his death. His three younger children married and settled here. The farm remained in Davis family possession for 225 years when Horace Davis visited in 1881. Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 17–21.
1666
–1673
Final Return to Barnstable & Second Marriage
After Margery's death and the marriages of his younger children, Dolor returned to Barnstable, readmitted as an inhabitant in 1666. He married a second time — Mrs. Joanna (Hull) Bursley, widow of Captain John Bursley — likely around 1671. There were no children by this marriage.
Second Wife
Joanna Hull Bursley Davis
b. 1620, England  ·  d. after 1683, Barnstable
Daughter of Rev. Joseph Hull; widow of Capt. John Bursley. She outlived Dolor and was still living on the Bursley homestead in 1683.
Sept. 13,
1672
His Last Will and Testament
Dolor dictated his will at Barnstable, describing himself as "weak and crazy in body but of perfect mind and memory." All Concord real estate went to his eldest son John — along with his carpenter's tools, serge suit, and cloak. Sons Simon I and Samuel had already received their portions during his lifetime. Daughter Mary Lewis received household goods; daughter Ruth Hall received a cow and fifty shillings. The formal inventory of his estate was recorded as £125 5s. 1d. — 125 pounds, 5 shillings, and one penny — a respectable sum representing several years' wages for a skilled craftsman, and a fitting measure of what a house-carpenter from Kent had built over forty years in the New World. Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 22–27. Plymouth Probate Records, July 2, 1673.
June
1673
Death at Barnstable
Dolor Davis died in Barnstable in June 1673. Given the revised birth estimate of before 1593, he was likely at least 80 years of age — older than Horace Davis's 1881 estimate of 73, which was based on the now-revised birth year. His will was proved at Plymouth on July 2, 1673. He was likely interred in the old burying-ground west of the town, but no stone marks his resting place. He left five confirmed living children — John, Mary, Simon I, Samuel, and Ruth — and thirty-four grandchildren. Source: Horace Davis (1881), pp. 26–27.

"As a man he was honest, industrious, and prudent; as a Christian tolerant and exact in the performance of his religious duties; as a neighbor kind, obliging, and ever ready to help those who needed his assistance; and as a father and the head of his family he was constantly solicitous for the welfare of all its members."

— Amos Otis, Barnstable Patriot, 1863
