Generation III

Simon Davis II

Of Bedford & Rutland, Massachusetts  ·  1683–1763
Dolor Davis→ Samuel Davis→ Simon Davis II (b. 1683)→ Simon Davis III (b. 1714)→ Isaac Davis→ John Davis→ Horace Davis

Third in the Horace line, Simon Davis II was born into a Massachusetts still raw from the devastation of King Philip's War. He grew up in Bedford, inherited land from his father Samuel, and in his middle years made the move inland to the frontier town of Rutland — carrying the Davis name deeper into the Massachusetts interior.

Simon Davis II, Lieutenant

Horace Line · Generation III Simon II is the sixth child of Samuel Davis and Mary Meddowes, and the great-grandson of Dolor Davis. He carries the line from Bedford to Rutland, where his son Simon III will continue it into the mid-18th century.
Aug. 9,
1683
Birth in Bedford, Massachusetts
Simon Davis II was born on August 9, 1683, on the meadow-edge farm in what was then western Concord, Massachusetts — land his father Samuel had received from Dolor Davis and which would eventually become the separate township of Bedford. He was the sixth of Samuel and Mary Meddowes Davis's seven children, born into a household shaped by the privations of frontier colonial life.
Historical Context · 1683
Simon II was born just eight years after King Philip's War (1675–76) — the bloodiest per-capita conflict in American history — had laid waste to much of New England. His uncle, Simon Davis I (Lieut.), had commanded troops in that war after Capt. Wheeler was wounded at Brookfield. The towns of the Massachusetts interior, including Concord and its surrounds, had lived under constant threat of attack throughout Simon II's earliest years. The landscape he grew up in was one of slow rebuilding.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 41. Concord Town Records.
1713
Marriage to Dorothy ——
In 1713, Simon II married Dorothy, whose maiden name has not been recovered in the records available to Horace Davis. She was born around 1692 based on her age at death, and she outlived Simon II by thirteen years.

Dorothy's maiden name was not recorded by Horace Davis, who noted the gap in the record with candor. She bore Simon II eight children over more than two decades, and outlived him by thirteen years — dying in 1776, the year the colonies declared independence, in her 84th year.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 41.
circa1720
Removal to Rutland, Massachusetts
Around 1720, Simon II and Dorothy moved from Bedford to Rutland, Massachusetts — a frontier township in Worcester County, incorporated in 1714 and still sparsely settled when the Davis family arrived. The move was typical of the Davis pattern established by Dolor himself: following opportunity and open land into newer settlements.

Rutland sat at the geographic heart of Massachusetts, roughly forty miles west of Boston, surrounded by rolling hills and dense forest. In the early 18th century it was still considered the interior frontier, with Worcester the nearest significant town to the south.
Rutland, Massachusetts — the frontier township to which Simon Davis II moved around 1720. Incorporated in 1714, it was one of the newer settlements of the Massachusetts interior when the Davis family arrived.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 41.
by
1714
Land Settled by His Father Samuel
As was the practice established by Dolor Davis and continued by Samuel, Simon received his portion of the family's Concord/Bedford lands from his father Samuel during Samuel's own lifetime — before 1714, when Samuel was last recorded living. Ancient deeds preserved by the Davis family confirm this transfer. Simon, like his father and grandfather before him, styled himself a husbandman — a working farmer and landowner. Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 40.
early
1700s
Military Rank — Lieutenant
Simon II held the rank of Lieutenant in the colonial militia — likely earned through service in the ongoing frontier conflicts of the early 18th century, including Queen Anne's War (1702–1713), which brought renewed raids on Massachusetts interior towns not far from Rutland. The title Lieutenant distinguished him in community records and was used consistently in the family's genealogical accounts, reflecting the civic standing that military rank conferred in colonial New England.
Colonial Massachusetts militia — the type of company in which Simon would have served and earned his lieutenant's rank.
Worcester County, Massachusetts — home to both Rutland and the later Davis family branches, spanning the interior of colonial Massachusetts.
1714
–1752
Eight Children in Rutland
Simon II and Dorothy had eight children who spread across the Worcester County towns of Holden, Paxton, Leicester, and Princeton. His eldest son Simon III (b. May 17, 1714) remained in Rutland, married Hannah Gates of Stow, and had eleven children of his own — and it is through Simon III that the Samuel Davis Line continues. Horace Davis wrote of Simon III and Hannah: "They are my ancestors."

His son Joseph (b. July 16, 1720) entered Harvard College in 1740 and served as minister at Holden from 1742 to 1773 — the first clergyman in the Davis line.
Feb. 16,
1763
Death at Rutland
Simon Davis II died on February 16, 1763, in Rutland, at approximately 79 years of age. His wife Dorothy survived him by thirteen years, dying on July 21, 1776, in the 84th year of her age. Both are buried in the Holden graveyard — one of the few concrete physical markers remaining for this generation of the family.

At his death, Simon II had seen Massachusetts transformed from a beleaguered frontier colony into a prosperous province on the verge of revolution. His grandchildren would live through the war his generation had made possible.
A colonial-era burying ground of the type where Simon and Dorothy Davis lie in Holden, Massachusetts — their gravestones among the earliest physical Davis family monuments in Worcester County.
Source: Horace Davis (1881), p. 41.

The Northborough Branch
The Horace Davis book devotes particular attention to the Northborough Branch — the line that descends from Simon II (b. 1683) through his eldest son Simon Davis III (b. 1714, m. Hannah Gates), and then through Simon III's son Isaac Davis (b. 1749), who removed to Westborough and then to Northborough, Massachusetts. Isaac became a deacon and a seven-term member of the Massachusetts Legislature, and his son John Davis rose to become Governor of Massachusetts and a U.S. Senator — the most prominent member of the Davis family in American public life. Horace Davis was John's son, and it is through this line that the chronicle was written.

San Francisco, California · 1881

The Davis family, now in its third American generation, had moved from the immigrant's grant in Cambridge to the Cape, from the Cape to Concord, and now from Concord's meadows inland to the hills of Worcester County. Each generation pressed a little further into the Massachusetts interior — not from restlessness, but from the same quiet ambition that had brought Dolor across the Atlantic a century before.

— Editorial note, Davis Family Chronicle
